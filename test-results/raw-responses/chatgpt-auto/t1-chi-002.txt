For a Pearson chi-square test on a **3×2** table using **Cohen’s (w)**:

* Rows (=3), columns (=2)
* Degrees of freedom:
  [
  df=(3-1)(2-1)=2
  ]
* Critical value at (\alpha=0.05):
  [
  \chi^2_{0.95,,2}=5.991
  ]
* Under the alternative, the chi-square test statistic is approximately **noncentral chi-square** with
  [
  \lambda = N w^2
  ]
  Here (w=0.25 \Rightarrow w^2=0.0625), so (\lambda = 0.0625,N).

Power condition:
[
\text{Power} = P\left(\chi^2_{df=2,\ \lambda=Nw^2} \ge 5.991\right) = 0.80
]

Solving this gives the smallest integer (N) achieving at least 80% power:

[
\boxed{N \approx 155\ \text{total}}
]

Quick check at that (N):

* (\lambda = 155 \times 0.0625 = 9.6875)
* Power (\approx 0.802) (just over 0.80)

If you plan **equal allocation across the 3 treatment groups**, that’s about:

* (155/3 \approx 51.7) → roughly **52, 52, 51** participants per treatment group (total).

(And if you expect dropout/missing outcomes, inflate (N) accordingly.)
