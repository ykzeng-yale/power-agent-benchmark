To power a **one-sided test of a Pearson correlation**:

* (H_0:\rho = 0)
* (H_1:\rho > 0)

a common planning approach uses the **Fisher (z)-transform**.

## Step 1) Convert the target correlation to Fisher (z)

[
z(\rho)=\operatorname{atanh}(\rho)=\tfrac12\ln!\left(\frac{1+\rho}{1-\rho}\right)
]
With (\rho = 0.2):
[
z(0.2)=\tfrac12\ln\left(\frac{1.2}{0.8}\right)=\tfrac12\ln(1.5)\approx 0.20273
]

## Step 2) Get the normal critical values

* One-sided (\alpha=0.025 \Rightarrow z_{1-\alpha}=z_{0.975}\approx 1.95996)
* Power (=0.85 \Rightarrow \beta=0.15 \Rightarrow z_{1-\beta}=z_{0.85}\approx 1.03643)

## Step 3) Sample size formula (Fisher-z approximation)

For detecting (\rho\neq 0) (here one-sided, positive direction):
[
n \approx \left(\frac{z_{1-\alpha}+z_{1-\beta}}{z(\rho)}\right)^2 + 3
]

Plug in:
[
n \approx \left(\frac{1.95996+1.03643}{0.20273}\right)^2 + 3
= \left(\frac{2.99639}{0.20273}\right)^2 + 3
\approx (14.776)^2 + 3
\approx 218.45 + 3
\approx 221.45
]

## Result

You should plan for **(n = 222) patients total** (rounding up) to achieve **85% power** for detecting a **positive correlation of (r=0.2)** with a **one-sided (\alpha=0.025)**.

If you expect missingness/nonadherence in measurement, you’d typically inflate this (e.g., 10% attrition → (222/0.90 \approx 247)).
