To power a test of **correlation = 0** (H₀: ρ = 0) when you expect a true correlation of **r = 0.25**, the standard approach uses the **Fisher z-transform**.

### Step 1) Fisher z effect size

[
\delta ;=; \operatorname{atanh}(r);=;\frac12\ln\left(\frac{1+r}{1-r}\right)
]
For (r=0.25):
[
\delta = \operatorname{atanh}(0.25)=0.2554
]

### Step 2) Critical values

Assuming a **two-sided** test with (\alpha=0.05):
[
z_{1-\alpha/2}=z_{0.975}=1.960
]
For **90% power** ((1-\beta=0.90)):
[
z_{1-\beta}=z_{0.90}=1.282
]

### Step 3) Sample size formula

[
n ;=;\left(\frac{z_{1-\alpha/2}+z_{1-\beta}}{\delta}\right)^2 + 3
]
Plug in:
[
n=\left(\frac{1.960+1.282}{0.2554}\right)^2+3
=\left(\frac{3.242}{0.2554}\right)^2+3
\approx 164.07
]

### Result (rounded up)

[
\boxed{n = 165\text{ participants}}
]

That’s the total number of participants needed to have **90% power** to detect a true correlation of **0.25** at **two-sided (\alpha=0.05)**.

*If you meant a one-sided (\alpha=0.05), it drops to about (\boxed{135}) participants, but most correlation analyses use two-sided unless there’s a strong preregistered directional claim.*
