We can treat this as a **cohort / cross-sectional** comparison of disease risk between **exposed vs unexposed** (binary exposure), with a target **odds ratio (OR) = 0.6**, baseline risk in unexposed **p₀ = 0.25**, and exposure prevalence **50%** → approximately **equal group sizes**.

## 1) Convert the OR into an expected risk in the exposed group

Odds in unexposed:
[
\text{odds}_0=\frac{p_0}{1-p_0}=\frac{0.25}{0.75}=\frac13
]

With a protective OR = 0.6:
[
\text{odds}_1 = 0.6 \cdot \text{odds}_0 = 0.6\cdot\frac13=\frac{0.6}{3}=0.2
]

Convert odds back to probability:
[
p_1=\frac{\text{odds}_1}{1+\text{odds}_1}=\frac{0.2}{1.2}=\frac16\approx 0.1667
]

So you expect:

* **Unexposed risk:** (p_0=0.25)
* **Exposed risk:** (p_1\approx 0.1667)
* Absolute difference: (\Delta = p_1-p_0 \approx -0.0833)

## 2) Sample size for two independent proportions (equal n per group)

With two-sided (\alpha=0.05): (z_{1-\alpha/2}=1.96)
With power (1-\beta=0.90): (z_{1-\beta}=1.2816)

Let (\bar p=(p_0+p_1)/2). For equal groups, an approximate required **n per group** is:
[
n \approx
\frac{\left[
z_{1-\alpha/2}\sqrt{2\bar p(1-\bar p)}+
z_{1-\beta}\sqrt{p_0(1-p_0)+p_1(1-p_1)}
\right]^2}{(p_1-p_0)^2}
]

Plug in:

* (\bar p=(0.25+0.1667)/2=0.2083)
* (\sqrt{2\bar p(1-\bar p)} \approx \sqrt{2(0.2083)(0.7917)} \approx 0.574)
* (\sqrt{p_0(1-p_0)+p_1(1-p_1)} \approx \sqrt{0.25(0.75)+0.1667(0.8333)} \approx 0.571)
* (|p_1-p_0|=0.0833)

Numerator:
[
(1.96\cdot0.574 + 1.2816\cdot0.571)^2
\approx (1.125 + 0.732)^2
\approx (1.857)^2
\approx 3.45
]
Denominator:
[
(0.0833)^2\approx 0.00694
]
So:
[
n \approx 3.45/0.00694 \approx 497
]

## Answer

* **About 500 participants per group** (exposed and unexposed)
* **Total sample size ≈ 1,000**

### If you want to inflate for attrition / missingness

* 10% loss: (1000/0.9 \approx 1111) → **~1,120 total** (≈560/group)
* 15% loss: (1000/0.85 \approx 1176) → **~1,180 total** (≈590/group)

If your planned analysis is **logistic regression with covariates** (vs a simple two-group comparison), the needed N can shift a bit (often *up* if the exposure prevalence effectively drifts from 50/50 or if you anticipate model complexity).
