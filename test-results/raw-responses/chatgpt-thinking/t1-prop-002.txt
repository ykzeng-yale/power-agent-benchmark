Assume a **two-sided** test at (\alpha=0.05), equal allocation, and we want to detect a drop from
(p_C=0.30) to (p_T=0.20) ((\Delta = 0.10)) with **80% power**.

A common planning formula for two independent proportions is:

[
n ;=; \frac{\Big[z_{1-\alpha/2}\sqrt{2\bar p(1-\bar p)} ;+; z_{1-\beta}\sqrt{p_C(1-p_C)+p_T(1-p_T)}\Big]^2}{(p_C-p_T)^2},
\quad \bar p=\frac{p_C+p_T}{2}
]

Plug in:

* (z_{1-\alpha/2}=1.96) (for (\alpha=0.05) two-sided)
* (z_{1-\beta}=0.84) (for 80% power)
* (\bar p=(0.30+0.20)/2=0.25)

Compute the pieces:

* (\sqrt{2\bar p(1-\bar p)}=\sqrt{2\cdot0.25\cdot0.75}=\sqrt{0.375}=0.612)
* (\sqrt{p_C(1-p_C)+p_T(1-p_T)}=\sqrt{0.3\cdot0.7+0.2\cdot0.8}=\sqrt{0.37}=0.608)

Then:
[
n \approx \frac{(1.96\cdot0.612 + 0.84\cdot0.608)^2}{0.10^2}
= \frac{(1.199 + 0.511)^2}{0.01}
= \frac{1.710^2}{0.01}
\approx \frac{2.92}{0.01}
\approx 292
]

**Answer: (\boxed{292 \text{ patients per arm}})** (round up; many people would report **~293 per arm** to be safe).

If you expect dropout/nonadherence, inflate: e.g., **10% attrition** (\Rightarrow 292/0.9 \approx 325) per arm.
