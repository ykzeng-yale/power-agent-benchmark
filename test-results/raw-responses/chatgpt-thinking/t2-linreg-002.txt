With **one predictor** in a linear regression, “explains 15% of the variance” means

* (R^2 = 0.15)
* In simple linear regression, (R^2 = r^2), so (r = \sqrt{0.15} \approx 0.387)

Testing whether the biomarker predicts severity is equivalent to testing whether the population correlation/slope is nonzero. A common planning formula uses Fisher’s (z) transform:

[
n \approx \left(\frac{z_{1-\alpha/2} + z_{1-\beta}}{\operatorname{atanh}(r)}\right)^2 + 3
]

Plug in:

* (\alpha=0.05) two-sided (\Rightarrow z_{1-\alpha/2} = 1.96)
* Power (=0.90 \Rightarrow \beta=0.10 \Rightarrow z_{1-\beta}=1.2816)
* (r\approx 0.387 \Rightarrow \operatorname{atanh}(r)=\tfrac12\ln!\left(\frac{1+r}{1-r}\right)\approx 0.409)

Compute:

[
n \approx \left(\frac{1.96+1.2816}{0.409}\right)^2 + 3
\approx (7.9)^2 + 3
\approx 66
]

**Answer:** you need **about *66 patients*** to detect (R^2 \approx 0.15) with **90% power** at **(\alpha=0.05)** (two-sided).

If you expect missingness/unevaluable outcomes, inflate: e.g., 10% loss (\Rightarrow 66/0.90 \approx 74) → **~74 patients**.
